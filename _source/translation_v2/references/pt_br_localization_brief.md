Target: publishable Brazilian Portuguese technical-editorial prose.

This is localization, not sentence transport. The translated text must read as if the same author had written it directly in Portuguese for Brazilian readers.

Method:
- Preserve meaning, voice, argument structure, caveats, and humor.
- Rebuild sentence movement, connective tissue, and clause order when literal transfer sounds imported from English.
- Prefer the wording a Brazilian technical writer would naturally publish, even when that requires full sentence rewrites.

Borrowings and foreignisms:
- Decide borrowings artifact-wide, not occurrence by occurrence.
- Keep English when Brazilian technical usage genuinely expects the English form.
- Localize when Brazilian editorial usage naturally prefers Portuguese.
- Keep product names, platform names, branded terms, and hard identifiers exactly as named.
- Avoid socially wrong half-decisions, such as translating a term in one paragraph and borrowing it in the next without a contextual reason.

Punctuation and rhythm:
- Normalize punctuation to PT-BR editorial usage instead of copying English pauses.
- Use the travessão only when it genuinely improves the sentence; do not abuse it.
- Do not use a comma before the opening travessão in inciso structures.
- Recast overlong English sentences when Brazilian Portuguese would read better with different clause structure or cadence.

Discourse and rhetoric:
- Preserve argumentative layering: a sentence may be stating a fact, softening a disagreement, making a joke, and framing credibility at the same time.
- Do not flatten connective texture into generic explanatory prose.
- Preserve restrained emphasis, contrast, caveats, and understated irony when present.

Register:
- Serious, readable, technically precise Brazilian prose.
- Not bureaucratic officialese.
- Not generic neutral “LLM Portuguese”.
- Not literal English with Portuguese words.

Common failure patterns to avoid:
- literal calques like "puxar fios" for "pull threads"
- clause ordering that feels copied from English
- punctuation that reflects English pacing instead of Portuguese syntax
- mixed English/Portuguese term handling within the same artifact
- prose that is understandable but does not sound like it belongs to the Brazilian language community
