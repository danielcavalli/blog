# Writing Style Reference — Daniel Cavalli

## Voice

Opinionated, layered, structurally aware, dry. The surface reads as straightforward
technical opinion, but there is usually a second thing happening underneath. Functions
that most writers separate into different sentences (disclaiming, establishing
credibility, being funny) happen simultaneously in the same breath.

Example — the lawyers joke in "AI Agents and the Illusion of Continuity":

> And following the advice of my lawyers, I should say that all of this is a personal
> opinion. An informed one, perhaps. I use these tools daily and spend a fair amount of
> time researching how the models behind them work. An opinion nonetheless.

This is a disclaimer, a joke, and a credibility move all at once. The reader gets all
three without any of them being announced.

## Humor

British-ironic. Dry. Jokes do not signal themselves. They land a few seconds late, if
the reader is paying attention.

The humor works in layers. A typical pattern:

1. A statement that reads straight but contains a self-deprecating setup.
2. A second statement that confirms the joke, making it almost explicit.
3. A third statement that recontextualizes both, adding a twist.

Example from "Don't Outsource the Thinking":

> I do think really good software developers (much better than me) will still code by
> hand from time to time. At least for a time, while AI still hasn't quiet got to their
> level. Since I am not one of those programmers, I've been an avid adopter of AI since
> around 2022!

Layer 1: "really good software developers (much better than me)" — self-deprecation,
reads as modesty. Layer 2: "Since I am not one of those programmers" — confirms the
joke, makes it almost explicit. Layer 3: "I've been an avid adopter of AI since around
2022" — the twist. Not only is he not among the great programmers who haven't been
replaced, he's been replaced since 2022. Multiple jokes stacked without ever saying
"this is a joke."

Arguments follow the same layered pattern. What looks like a throwaway line is often
load-bearing.

## Argument Structure

Strong instinct for structure as argument. Each paragraph must earn the next. Sections
that are internally fine but poorly connected are caught and reworked.

The preferred pattern for introducing a claim the reader might resist:

1. **Validate the reader's instinct.** Assume they've already thought about this and
   arrived at the obvious conclusion. Meet their intelligence.
2. **Ground it.** Explain why that instinct is reasonable, often with an analogy or
   human parallel.
3. **Pivot.** Redirect — not by dismissing their view, but by showing where it stops
   working.
4. **Land the actual claim.** Now the reader is ready for it.

Example from "AI Agents and the Illusion of Continuity":

> Most people's first instinct is to keep more history alive. A reasonable instinct. Any
> human learning a subject would take on as much information as possible to derive their
> conclusion, so it is only logical that providing more information to an agent would
> result in better reasoning. Past a point, though, the opposite happens.

The reader is brought along rather than taught. Intelligence is assumed and met with
guided text that leads to the conclusion.

## Tone Guardrails

- Direct, not theatrical.
- Respectful, not deferential.
- Precise, not mystical.
- Technical, but not detached from real workflow.
- First person, blog voice rather than impersonal documentation.
- Analytic and confident, but fair to opposing framings.
- Explanatory before prescriptive.
- Critique claims and models, not people.
- No em dashes. Prefer proper grammatical punctuation (periods, commas, colons).
- Connective tissue matters. Sentences should flow into each other, not sit as
  isolated fragments. Choppy sequences of short sentences are a sign of under-editing.

## What to Avoid

- Announcing disagreement instead of just disagreeing ("This is where I part ways
  with...").
- Academic scaffolding that declares structure instead of moving forward ("The first
  distinction that matters is...").
- Over-polishing that sands off personality. The writing should sound like a specific
  person, not a well-edited committee output.
- Repeating a thesis that was already established in a previous post. Each piece should
  advance the argument, not re-litigate it.
- Emojis. Never.

## Language Notes

Primary writing language is English, with Portuguese (BR) as native language. First
drafts in English sometimes lose connective tissue between ideas — the rhythm of
Portuguese doesn't always map 1:1 to English prose. Watch for sentences that contain
the right ideas but don't flow into each other naturally. The instinct to keep things
tight is good, but occasionally a thought needs one more beat to land.
